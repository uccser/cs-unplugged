Teaching CS Unplugged at a distance

{% endblock page_heading %} {% block content_container %}

CS Unplugged at a Distance is a series of learning opportunities to grow your participants’ understanding of computer science concepts and how they relate to their world, whether your participants are students in a class, or teachers attending professional development.

CS Unplugged at a Distance is an adaptation of the CS Unplugged activities to support teachers faced with delivering teaching online, and those delivering professional development virtually. The goal is for participants to have a close approximation to the usual in-person experience, for teachers learning about Unplugged, helping them to understand how teaching CS Unplugged face-to-face would work even if they aren’t able to experience it directly at the time. It is a series of learning opportunities to grow your participants’ understanding of computer science concepts and how they relate to their world, whether your participants are students in a classroom, or teachers attending professional development.

These activities are based on live sessions using video conference systems such as Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams.

Disclaimer: The software, applications and equipment listed in this section and in the lessons are all tools that we used in 2021 - 2022 while developing these resources. We have carefully considered the functionality that you need from these tools so that as new tools become available you are able to make an informed decision to use them.

Age appropriateness of lessons

Many of these lessons state they are appropriate for ages 5+ years. This does not mean that it is not worthwhile for older participants (even secondary school aged) to do these lessons. More capable participants are likely to move through the lessons faster, but it is definitely worthwhile for them to do these same activities to support the computer science concepts taught through them.

General tips for teaching CS Unplugged at a distance

This section summarises some general principles about transferring an engaging face-to-face experience to an online context; these were developed in the course of experimenting with different ways of delivering Unplugged online.

Orientation with the video conferencing software

At the start of each session it’s good to ask some ice-breaker questions while people are joining your session. If any participants can’t respond in the intended matter it’s a good time to help them understand how to use the video conferencing software. Sometimes different versions of the software may mean that some participants don’t have the same features available, or don’t see the same layout, so you may need to adjust your presentation to take account of that.

Icebreaker example questions

  • Start with a simple question such as “Where are you joining us from?” or even “What’s your favourite colour?” and have them reply in the chat.
  • Send an emoji in the chat; to give this purpose, you could ask them to send one that reflects how their day is going.
  • Explore the reactions feature (typically a thumbs up/down or hand up) by asking questions like “do you like ice cream?” and “are you ready for a holiday?” If these aren’t available in your system, participants can use a physical thumbs up/down with their video on, or with text in the chat.

Facilitation notes

Each of your participants may have differing levels of confidence using their device. Be aware that the layout, menus and features may be different between platforms and devices, such as iPads, tablets, Windows, MacOs (Apple) and mobile phones.

Using the chat functionality is usually an effective way to engage participants. We have found it to be an excellent primary method of getting audience feedback. As a facilitator, be mindful that it does take time for your participants to think of an answer, type it, and for it to appear in the chat. The response time depends on the question, and it could take up to 20 seconds before answers start to appear. It’s important to not speak during that time to allow people to think. If it’s a small group, open the discussion by unmuting your participants’ mics and ask them to indicate they have something to add by raising their hand.

Breakout rooms can sometimes be useful during large online workshop presentations, but we have written these resources to keep the participants together online in the same room. Using breakout rooms can require some experience for them to be effective, and can depend on having people available to facilitate them if you have a lot of participants. The time it takes to organise people into the breakout rooms can interrupt the flow of a session, but if you’re confident using them, they can be used with discretion to manage the activities.

Likewise, polls and reactions are often available in video conference systems, but these can also be disruptive to the flow of the presentation, despite being an obvious way to get feedback.

If participants have cameras available, they are comfortable having them on, and you can view all the participants' cameras at the same time, participants could respond through their camera. You could ask them to have a pad and paper ready and they hold up the answers in front of the camera. Alternatively, small numbers could be indicated by holding up that number of fingers, and yes/no answers could be indicated with a physical thumbs up/down gesture. Although these can all be done using online reactions and chat, using video like this can introduce slightly more physical engagement and makes responding less dependent on navigating the video interface.

It’s good if your online platform is set up so that you can mute individuals and mute the entire group, otherwise online platforms tend to switch to the participants who are speaking (including background noises) which can interrupt the flow of your session. Be aware that if a participant is wearing noise cancelling headphones they may not be aware of the noise around them that other participants in your session may be able to hear if they are not on mute.

Sharing slides and screen sharing can make seeing your own speaker notes complicated. If you don't have the ability to do this easily, we recommend printing the provided speaker notes. This allows you to focus on facilitating and following the suggested script and facilitation notes while avoiding the audience seeing them.

Preparation and planning checklist

This section supports you to plan and prepare for your CS Unplugged at a Distance session. The answers to these questions will support you to meet the learning needs of your participants. This process will also highlight what information you should include when promoting your session, for example, the online platform you are using and what equipment participants should bring to the session.

How will your participants be accessing this session?

The reason we ask this question is that it affects how you ask people to respond - if multiple people are viewing on the same screen then they may not be able to answer individually. The language you use in the session will help ensure everyone feels that they are involved e.g. asking a representative of each group to send responses to questions.

Consider the different ways people may be viewing the session:

  • Each person signing in from their own device: how will you let them know that the screen needs to be large enough to be able to view what is happening on the screen and access the ‘chat’ feature of the platform at the same time?
  • One screen (TV or data projector), camera and audio system with a group of 2 or more participants viewing it, with one person typing responses.
  • A shared screen and audio system with a group of 2 or more participants signing in from their own device (to interact from their own device, in which case they need to make sure their microphone and speaker are muted on their own device).

Which online platform will you use?

Some organisations and schools only allow one platform to be used, and some will block access to other platforms. Participants may need to gain permission in advance to access your session, or access it from a home network where access isn’t blocked. You may want to set up a meeting in advance for those wanting to check if they can access the online platform.

  • Zoom
  • Microsoft Teams
  • Google Meet

What access will you give your participants?

Controlling access can be useful for security, but it can also mean that late arrivals might be disruptive if they need to be admitted, or arrive with the microphone turned on. It’s good to allow for early arrivals; this can be a chance to connect with some of the audience.

Consider how arrivals will access the session:

  • Wait to be admitted?
  • Be in a waiting room until you arrive?
  • Join the online platform room when they click on the link?
  • Muted and cameras off when they join?

How will your participants interact with this session?

Encouraging feedback and participation is key to making online sessions engaging. Platforms offer a variety of options for this; “chat” is generally the most immediate, and in our experience the best to use because participants can respond quickly. If you’re asking for a vote, it may not be private or completely accurate to have them vote using chat, but the immediacy can outweigh that, and participants can get a strong sense of responses as they see them go past in the chat window. If you were there in person, you might have asked for a show of hands, and that wouldn’t be private or completely accurate either.

Some of the Unplugged activities would seem to work well with polls and audience responses systems, but these can take some time to set up, and you often have to wait until everyone has responded, which can interrupt the flow of the session.

When taking into account which approach to use, considerations will include the cost (to you and your participants) and immediacy. Sometimes “trial” versions of software have limitations (such as the length of a meeting) that can badly interrupt the flow of the event. Also, try to use features built into the video conference system to avoid asking people to install more software.

One point to be aware of with some chat features is that often the previous content isn’t available to people who join late; for example, if you post a link to a web page that they should be looking at, late arrivees may not be able to see the link. Look for features such as “pinning” information.

Spend time looking at how you can customise your meetings to meet your specific session needs, and if you’re not familiar with the system, experiment with a friend acting as the audience.

A list of features and tools we have used are below. Which ones are you considering for your session?

  • Chat
  • Polls
  • Reactions
  • Using emojis in the chat
  • Verbal through microphone
  • Whiteboard
  • Shared platform - such as a shared document or slides that participants can access information from, or even contribute to
  • Audience response systems such as Socrative, Mentimeter, Kahoot!, Slido, Poll Everywhere

Which online presentation and interactive tool(s) are you considering using for this session?

The following tools provide alternative solutions to share information with participants, and allow them to share their own thoughts and ideas. Some options place some trust on participants to not remove material added by other participants, while others keep track of each individual’s contribution. Remember to check the sharing rights of any files. To check to see if people will have access to files you will be sharing, use the ‘incognito’ feature on your web browser and then open the link you will be sharing with your participants.

Put time aside to look at the features of the tools you have chosen and consider which would be best for answering your questions. Are you wanting to be able to go back to the answers at the end of the session or in future sessions? If the answer is yes, then consider an application like Padlet or Jamboard; if it’s no, then chat may be the simplest way to do it.

Are you recording this session?

Sometimes people will ask you to record a session, although this raises privacy risks (including surprise walk-ins), can inhibit questions and conversations, and people watching later miss out on the engagement that attendees would have. If someone can’t be there live, it may be better to offer an alternative repeat session, or post a summary in a different format that’s more suitable for them to read or view.

  • Yes, I have taken the participants’ privacy and willingness to participate into account.
  • No, I’m not recording this session.

How are you taking into account privacy for your participants including but not limited to:

  • Background and where the participants are
  • Encouraging people to have their camera on but not requiring it
  • Conversations happening in the background
  • Referring to participants by name or location
  • Participants giving consent to be recorded

After the session you can record a separate summary or edit out conversations of the recorded session. Bear in mind that if the chat is an important part of the session, it may need to be recorded, and if it can’t, the commentary could be used to explain what is happening to people watching the recording e.g. “I see that most people have answered ‘yes’”. If you are using breakout rooms recording becomes problematic as you can’t usually record the conversations in the breakout rooms.

What is your back up plan if you as the facilitator loses access to the session while you are live?

  • Present with two facilitators, where both have full access (both are “hosts”). One person’s role could be to facilitate and the other as technical support and monitoring the chat. The support person can fill in if the main presenter is temporarily dropped off the session.
  • Have a mobile device fully charged, with internet connectivity, ready to log into the online platform in case your primary device fails.
  • Have a way to communicate with participants if the live session is terminated unexpectedly (e.g. have their email addresses at hand so you can quickly send information about what you will do).

It’s helpful to check what the host connection rights are in the online platform (for example, if the host drops out will the session automatically end?)
