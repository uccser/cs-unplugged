Share in the chat if you've felt like this using a digital system, where the interface (what it has displayed to you) is confusing to use.

Has a bad interface ever slowed you down or made you feel frustrated or even anxious?

This activity is called the 'Stroop effect' and is a famous experiment from psychology named after John Ridley Stroop. The Stroop test measures the amount of time it takes to say the name of the printed colours in an incongruent (incompatible, non-matching) list and compares this time to saying the name of the print colour in a congruent (agreeing, matching) list. Stroop found that people were often 2 or 3 times slower trying to read incongruent information.

Let's see it how this applies to Human Computer Interaction.

What for a couple of responses and respond appropriately. Answer: if people are in a hurry, they will naturally press the green button to continue (green means “go”, orange means “warning”), but that will wipe all the information they have entered.

{% include "at_a_distance/components/speech-bubble-open.html" %}

A lot of people pressed the wrong button and lost all their work. You could say that they didn't read it properly, but usually people are under pressure when they are using systems, and don't have time to carefully consider every step. If you expect them to do that, then it's like the Stroop effect. A good interface shouldn't make people have to think hard.

These are examples of just one type of problem in interfaces: confusing people with potentially contradictory messaging, even though they can work out what to do if they think about it. It only slows people down a little, but it can make them feel like you did when doing the Stroop experiment - frustrated and anxious, when they are trying to get their work done. Good interface designers anticipate and avoid these sorts of problems.

HCI: Answering our key question

Do software designers use psychology concepts when designing their applications?

{% include "at_a_distance/components/speech-bubble-open.html" %}

Software designers write programs for people, not for computers. We've shown in this session how important it is for them to consider and understand how their end users (people) will interact with the programs they create.

This kind of problem is just one of many ways that bad interfaces can make life difficult for computer users. There are many other ideas from psychology that are important in interface design, such as the length of delays people can handle waiting for software to respond (spoiler: one tenth of a second is the limit in a lot of cases), how inconsistent designs can be confusing, and lots more. Many of these things are fun when they are a game like the one we played, but can be very frustrating when the prevent someone from getting their job done.
